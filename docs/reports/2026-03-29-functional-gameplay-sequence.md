# Functional Gameplay Sequence

The project has reached a good transition point.

The game now has:

- a stable multi-biome world
- a journal and comparison loop
- day-part, weather, notebook prompts, and a field partner
- a newly widened `256x160` baseline that gives the playfield and overlays more breathing room

That means the next high-value shift should not be "more polish for polish's sake."

It should be a stronger play loop.

## Recommendation

After the current phenology slice stabilizes, move the next major priority toward functional gameplay:

1. deeper exploration geometry
2. notebook-style field requests
3. a tiny field-station upgrade loop

This should happen before:

- sketchbook mode
- broader soundscape work
- close-look inspect vignettes
- richer companion behavior

Those are still good features. They just are not the sharpest next step if the goal is to make the game feel more like a game.

## Why this is the right shift

Right now the game is strongest at:

- discovery
- atmosphere
- science framing
- light revisitation

It is weaker at:

- giving the player a short-term reason to move with intention
- making terrain itself feel rewarding to traverse
- creating a compact "I did something" loop beyond inspection and collection

The next systems should fix that without turning Eco Explorer into combat, grind, or a checklist-heavy quest game.

## What to build next

### 1. Exploration geometry first

Add more meaningful movement:

- a few more layered ledges and low routes
- at least one shallow cave, hollow, or underpass proof
- one small "go down, find something, come back up" detour

This is the best first step because it improves the core act of play before adding a new meta-system on top of it.

Guardrails:

- keep jumps readable and forgiving
- keep caves short and authored, not maze-like
- avoid combat, hazards, timers, or fail-state pressure
- use terrain to reward curiosity, not precision-platform mastery

Best first candidate:

- `forest` is the strongest first proof for a shallow cave, ravine, root tunnel, or log-hollow detour

It already supports layered ground, cover, nurse-log logic, and a more sheltered vertical feeling than beach or tundra.

### 2. Field requests, not quest logs

Once movement is richer, add tiny notebook-style requests:

- compare one shared species in two places
- look for two signs of shelter
- notice which plants stay low in wind
- find one lower hidden route

These should feel like a naturalist assignment, not chores.

Guardrails:

- no giant quest log
- no timer, streak, or score ladder
- no "collect ten things" filler
- keep requests few, calm, and tied to actual ecology concepts

The goal is to add purpose to exploration, not to overwrite the journal.

### 3. Field station instead of a literal sell-market

The user wants a market-like loop and upgrades. That is a good instinct, but the framing should stay science-safe.

Recommendation:

- do not build a system centered on selling living nature finds directly
- use a small field station, ranger cart, or research table instead
- let progress convert into tokens, stamps, or study credit through surveys and requests

Then use that gentle economy for:

- slightly faster walk speed
- a small jump improvement
- maybe a better map-return or inspect comfort upgrade later

This keeps the system playful without teaching extraction-first behavior from ecosystems.

## Deprioritized for now

These are still approved future features, but they should sit below the gameplay lane above:

- sketchbook mode
- broader biome soundscapes
- close-look inspect mode
- richer partner/companion expansion beyond the current quiet authored strip

## Suggested implementation order

1. Scout the functional game loop and recommend one coherent sequence.
2. Add an exploration-geometry proof with one shallow cave or underpass in one biome.
3. Review that proof for readability, flow, and kid-friendly difficulty.
4. Add notebook-style field requests using the journal and existing ecology data.
5. Review the request layer for tone and clutter.
6. Add a tiny field-station upgrade surface with one gentle movement upgrade.
7. Review that loop for ethics, pacing, and whether it still feels cozy.

## Key guardrails

- Keep science accuracy as a hard gate.
- Keep the tone cozy and observational.
- Prefer "field station" or "research cart" framing over direct wildlife selling.
- If an economy exists, it should reward surveys and observation, not extraction.
- Use the new `256x160` baseline to improve readability, but do not let the bigger frame justify HUD sprawl.
- Treat caves and vertical routes as authored moments of curiosity, not danger arenas.
- Keep upgrades modest and legible; they should smooth exploration, not transform the genre.
